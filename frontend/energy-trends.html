This module analyzes energy consumption patterns across different time scales (monthly, daily, hourly) and utility types to identify trends, peak usage periods, and anomalies. Understanding these patterns is essential for optimizing campus operations and planning energy reduction strategies.

Key Findings & Insights

üìà Finding: Seasonal Demand Variation

What We Discovered: Monthly energy consumption varies by 80B kWh between Jan-Apr 2025, with February showing peak demand at 2.35T kWh.

Why This Matters: February's cold temperatures drive heating loads (steam) to maximum, while April's moderate weather reduces demand. This 3.5% monthly swing represents millions in seasonal cost variations.

‚úÖ Solution: Pre-season HVAC tune-ups before November and May, thermal storage during off-peak hours, and occupancy-based heating schedules in moderate months can reduce peak demand by 15-20%.

üö® Finding: Extreme Energy Spikes

What We Discovered: 20 energy spikes occurred between April 1-5, 2025, all reaching 680B kWh‚Äîfar exceeding normal consumption patterns.

Why This Matters: These spikes cluster during late night/early morning hours (22:00-05:00), suggesting equipment malfunctions, data errors, or extreme weather events. The concentration in a 5-day window 1indicates a specific causal event.

‚úÖ Solution: Implement real-time anomaly detection alerts when consumption exceeds baseline by 50%+, investigate steam plant logs for April 1-5 period, install sub-meters on major equipment to isolate spike sources, and verify data quality from meters reporting during spike window.

‚ö° Finding: Steady Baseline Consumption

What We Discovered: Campus-wide energy consumption remains remarkably consistent at ~10.19M kWh throughout all hours, with less than 0.01% hourly variation.

Why This Matters: This near-flatline pattern suggests either data aggregation issues or that base loads (24/7 equipment like servers, refrigeration, ventilation) dominate usage, leaving little room for occupancy-driven variation. Normal campuses show 30-50% difference between peak and overnight hours.

‚úÖ Solution: If data quality is confirmed, this reveals massive waste from equipment running unnecessarily 24/7. Implement building-level scheduling (lights off 10PM-6AM), shut down HVAC in unoccupied buildings, install occupancy sensors, and power down non-critical equipment overnight. Expected savings: 20-30% by creating a proper load curve with lower overnight consumption.

üî• Finding: Heating Dominates Energy Mix

What We Discovered: Without complete utility breakdown data, January's peak consumption (2.27T kWh) strongly correlates with heating season, suggesting steam/heating represents the largest energy expenditure.

Why This Matters: If steam comprises 40-50% of total energy (typical for cold-climate universities), that's $500K-$1M+ annual spend. Every 1% efficiency gain in heating systems saves $5K-$10K yearly.

‚úÖ Solution: Deploy utility-specific metering to confirm breakdown, conduct steam system audit focusing on McCracken Power Plant efficiency, implement outdoor temperature reset controls (reduce heat output as weather warms), upgrade building envelopes on worst performers, and consider district geothermal heating for new construction. ROI: 3-7 years for major heating infrastructure upgrades.
